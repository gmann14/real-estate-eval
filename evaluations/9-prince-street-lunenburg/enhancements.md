## Property-Specific Context

Before recommending anything, here's what we're working with:

- **Heritage designation.** Lunenburg Old Town is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Exterior modifications need municipal heritage approval. Interior work is generally unrestricted, but anything visible from the street (windows, siding, additions) goes through the Heritage Advisory Committee.
- **Lot size: 0.0517 acres (~2,250 sq ft).** This is tiny. The building footprint plus gravel parking eats most of it. There's a private patio out back, but no room for an ADU, detached structure, or significant outdoor expansion.
- **Stone foundation, built 1761.** The oldest kind of building in Nova Scotia. Updated plumbing, electrical, roof, siding — but the bones are 265 years old. The full unfinished basement has a stone foundation with likely low ceiling height (6-6.5 ft typical for this era).
- **Character finishes.** Both units have heritage charm — "Behind The Hidden Door" and "The Crooked Door" branding already leans into the story. This is a feature, not a bug.
- **Location.** Walkable downtown Lunenburg. Waterfront, restaurants, shops, Bluenose II, Fisheries Museum all within 5-10 minute walk. This is the prime STR location in town.
- **Gravel parking.** 2 spots. Limited but adequate for Lunenburg (most visitors don't need a car once parked).
- **Heat pump + oil backup.** Already has the primary efficiency upgrade. Stone walls are the remaining thermal weak point.

### 9. Insulation Upgrade — Stone Foundation Focus

**Cost:** $8,000-$20,000 (interior insulation of basement walls + rim joist sealing)
**Impact:** 25-40% heating cost reduction ($700-$1,120/yr saved on $2,800 heating bill)
**Payback:** 8-18 years on energy savings alone

**For this property specifically:**

The 1761 stone foundation is the single biggest thermal liability. Stone walls have essentially zero insulation value (R-1 to R-3). Heat loss through the basement walls and floor above the basement is likely 30-40% of total building heat loss.

**Approach (heritage-compatible):**
1. **Interior spray foam on stone walls** (2-3 inches closed-cell): $5,000-$10,000. This is the standard for heritage stone foundations. Doesn't affect exterior appearance. Adds R-12 to R-18.
2. **Rim joist spray foam**: $1,000-$2,000. Where the wood frame sits on the stone — massive air leakage point.
3. **Basement ceiling insulation** (batt or spray): $2,000-$5,000. If the basement isn't being used as living space, insulating the ceiling keeps heat in the units above.

**Important:** Closed-cell spray foam on stone walls is the only approach that works. Open-cell foam or fiberglass batts against stone walls will trap moisture and cause the stone to deteriorate. This must be done correctly.

**Payback math:**
- Current heating: $2,800/yr (oil + heat pump electricity)
- After insulation: ~$1,700-$2,100/yr (30-40% reduction)
- Annual savings: $700-$1,100
- On a $12,000 investment: **11-17 year payback from energy savings alone**

**The real ROI is comfort and resilience, not just energy.** A warmer building in January means:
- Higher winter Airbnb occupancy (guests won't leave bad reviews about cold floors)
- Lower risk of frozen pipes (stone foundations in maritime climate)
- Lower stress on heating system = less maintenance

**Available rebates:**
- Efficiency Nova Scotia: up to $5,000 for insulation upgrades (requires energy audit ~$300)
- Federal Greener Homes Grant: up to $5,000 (if still available)
- Combined rebates could cut net cost to $5,000-$12,000

**Risk:** Low if done correctly (closed-cell spray foam by experienced contractor). High if done wrong — moisture problems in stone walls can cause structural damage over decades.

## Tier 3: Major Value-Adds ($25K+)

### 11. Basement Conversion to Third Unit

**Cost:** $60,000-$120,000
**Potential revenue:** +$800-$1,500/mo (LTR) or +$1,500-$2,500/mo (STR)
**Payback:** 36-72 months if feasible

**Feasibility for 9 Prince Street: LOW (20/100)**

This is the most valuable potential upgrade on paper and the hardest to execute on this specific property. Here's why:

**Ceiling height is likely a dealbreaker.** 1761 stone foundation basements in Lunenburg typically have 6-6.5 ft ceiling heights. NS Building Code requires minimum 6'5" (1.95m) for habitable space, and that's before any floor insulation or ceiling treatment. You'd need to verify actual height, but odds are it's too low without underpinning.

**Underpinning a stone foundation:** This means excavating below the existing foundation to create more headroom. On a 265-year-old stone foundation, this is:
- Extremely expensive ($40,000-$80,000 just for the underpinning)
- Structurally risky (the building has stood for 265 years; you're literally undermining its foundation)
- Requires a structural engineer experienced with heritage stone buildings
- Heritage committee would scrutinize this heavily

**Other barriers:**
- Egress: Code requires a window large enough to escape through. Below-grade stone walls make this difficult and expensive.
- Moisture: Stone foundations are inherently damp. Even with interior drainage and waterproofing, a habitable basement in a stone foundation building requires extensive moisture management.
- Services: Would need separate HVAC, electrical sub-panel, plumbing for kitchen and bath.
- Zoning: Town of Lunenburg would need to approve a triplex on a 0.0517-acre lot. Density may not be permitted.

**My take:** Don't do this. The structural risk and cost on a 1761 stone foundation are too high relative to the potential return. The foundation has survived 265 years — don't mess with it. Use the basement for storage, mechanicals, and laundry. If you want a third income stream, look at revenue strategy optimization (Tier 4) instead.

### 14. Solar Panels

**Feasibility for 9 Prince Street: POOR (25/100)**

- **Roof orientation:** Metal roof, but orientation and shading from adjacent heritage buildings need assessment. Heritage district = closely spaced buildings = shading issues.
- **Heritage approval:** Solar panels on a heritage roof in the UNESCO district will face significant scrutiny. Lunenburg has approved some solar installations, but each case is individual and panels must not be visible from key sightlines.
- **NS Power net metering:** Available, but NS electricity is relatively cheap (~$0.17/kWh). Payback on solar in NS is typically 12-18 years — among the longest in Canada.
- **Small roof area:** Duplex on a small lot = limited roof space = limited generation capacity.

**My take:** Solar doesn't make financial sense on this property. The combination of heritage restrictions, long payback period, and limited roof space makes this a poor investment. Spend the money on insulation instead — reducing consumption beats generating production in this case.

## 4. Structural Risks the Analysis Missed

### 🔴 Stone Foundation: Specific Failure Modes and Costs

The analysis mentions "get a heritage-specialist inspector" and "stone foundation from 1761" but doesn't quantify the actual risks. Here's what a 1761 stone foundation can do:

| Failure Mode | Likelihood | Cost to Remediate | Warning Signs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mortar deterioration** (repointing) | HIGH (almost certain over ownership) | $5,000-$20,000 depending on extent | Crumbling mortar between stones, white efflorescence |
| **Moisture penetration** | HIGH | $3,000-$15,000 (drainage, waterproofing) | Damp basement walls, musty smell, water stains |

| **Drainage failure** | Medium | $5,000-$20,000 (perimeter drain) | Standing water in basement, hydrostatic pressure |
